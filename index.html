01 — About

A quality analyst's instinct, pointed at AI.

I spent more than a decade making work measurable — building quality-assurance frameworks, scoring outputs against multi-point criteria, and turning loose processes into repeatable systems. Most recently I've run operations for C-suite leaders, where the same discipline keeps governance, decisions, and follow-through on track.

That habit transfers cleanly to AI. The hard part of working with generative models isn't generating — it's judging: deciding whether an output is accurate, complete, on-instruction, and safe to use. That's evaluation, and it's what I've always done. I use AI tools daily and treat every output as a draft to be reviewed, not a result to be trusted.

The samples below are how I think on paper: the rubrics, prompts, workflows, and checks I build to make AI-assisted work reliable.

AI Output Evaluation Rubric

A reusable rubric for scoring an AI-generated response before anyone relies on it. Each criterion is scored 0–2 with evidence — the same evidence-based scoring I used reviewing insurance claims at WNS, adapted for model output.

AccuracyClaims are factually correct and grounded; nothing invented.0 · 1 · 2
CompletenessAnswers the whole request; no missing parts or steps.0 · 1 · 2
Instruction adherenceFollows format, scope, and constraints that were asked for.0 · 1 · 2

Sample 06
Worked AI Evaluation — Preference Scoring
Evaluation in practice

A real evaluation run: two AI responses to the same brief, each scored against my rubric, then ranked. This is the core of LLM quality work — catching factual errors, instruction misses, and tone issues, then justifying a preference with evidence.

The brief given to the modelA customer asks why their motor windscreen claim was only partly paid. Draft an empathetic reply under 150 words: explain the policy's £75 glass excess, confirm the remainder was paid, and invite them to call. Do not invent policy details.
Response ANot preferred
“…a £100 excess applies to glass claims, so the balance was settled. Your claim ref CLM-4471 is now closed…”
Accuracy 0Complete 2Instruction 1Tone 2Safety 2
Response BPreferred
“…your policy carries a £75 excess on glass claims, which is why part wasn't covered. The remaining amount has been paid…”
Accuracy 2Complete 2Instruction 2Tone 1Safety 2
Verdict & rationale — Response B wins
  • Accuracy (the deciding fault): A states a £100 excess, contradicting the £75 brief, and invents claim ref CLM-4471 — two fabrications a customer would act on.
  • Instruction adherence: the brief said don't invent details; A added an unstated reference number, B stayed in scope and under length.
  • Tone: B is accurate but slightly procedural — one warmer line would earn the full 2.
  • Decision: a factual error that misleads a customer outweighs a minor tone gap, so B is preferred.
